Case Study 01

The AI Customer Center

A 0→1 vision for a unified, agentic end-customer portal — where buyers and customers self-serve billing, manage support, and engage with an AI agent without ever picking up a phone.

Role Staff Product Designer, Vision Lead Timeline 18 months Platform B2B SaaS · Customer Portal Status Vision + Phase 1 shipped Tags Vision · Agentic UX · 0→1 · Portal Design

The Problem

Fragmented touchpoints, unified frustration

Enterprise customers were navigating three separate surfaces to do what should be one job: check their invoice, update a payment method, file a support ticket, and get a real answer. Each surface had its own login, its own mental model, and its own dead end.

Support volume was high not because the product was broken — but because customers couldn't find answers without a human in the loop. The billing and support experience was built for internal ops, not for the customer doing the task.

The opportunity was to design a unified portal that collapsed these three surfaces into one, and then go one step further: let an AI agent handle the questions that shouldn't require a human at all.

My Role

Vision lead, not just designer

I led the end-to-end product vision for this initiative — from the first whiteboard session to the north star prototype presented to the executive team. This wasn't a project handed to me with a brief; I scoped the problem, made the case for the investment, and built the coalition to move it forward.

What I owned

Product vision, research synthesis, end-to-end UX, north star prototype, exec presentation

Who I partnered with

Product management, engineering leads, support ops, billing, and AI platform teams

How it was staffed

Solo design lead on vision; paired with 2 senior designers for execution phases

Evolution

How the work changed over time

Phase 01 Q1 2024

Mapping the Chaos

We started with research — 14 customer interviews, support ticket analysis, and journey mapping across billing, support, and account management. The finding that changed everything: customers weren't confused about features, they were confused about where they were. Every touchpoint felt like a different product.

Phase 02 Q2 2024

The First Vision

I built the first north star concept: a single portal shell with three modules — billing, support, and account management — connected by a shared navigation and a unified customer identity. Early designs were deliberately simple. We needed stakeholders to react to structure before getting lost in UI details.

▶ View prototype — Phase 1 concept Figma · Early portal structure · No interaction yet
Phase 03 Q3 2024

Adding the Agentic Layer

We ran a second round of research specifically around support interactions. The pattern was clear: 60% of tickets were answerable by a well-designed AI agent if it had access to billing context, order history, and account state. I designed the agent interaction model — how it surfaces in the portal, when it hands off to a human, and how it signals its confidence to the customer.

▶ View prototype — Agent interaction Figma · AI agent flow · Human handoff logic
Phase 04 Q4 2024

The Unified Surface

The final north star: billing, support, and agent converged into a single, coherent product experience. A customer can pay an invoice, file a ticket, and get a real-time answer from the AI agent — all without leaving the portal or switching context. This was the version presented to the exec team and approved for phased delivery.

Key Decisions

Three choices that defined the design

Single surface vs. tabbed navigation

We considered

A tabbed approach — each module (billing, support, account) as a separate tab, maintaining the existing IA.

We chose

A single unified surface with contextual modules that appear based on customer need.

Because

Customers were less confused about features and more about where they were. Tabs preserved exactly that cognitive split.

Human handoff vs. pure agent resolution

We considered

Having the agent attempt to resolve everything and only escalate to a human on failure.

We chose

Proactive handoff indicators — the agent signals when a human would do better, before the customer hits a wall.

Because

Trust is fragile. A failed AI resolution costs more relationship than a graceful handoff that earns confidence.

Real-time billing vs. async context

We considered

Showing billing data asynchronously to reduce load complexity and API dependency.

We chose

Real-time billing state with a clear loading contract — live data or an honest placeholder.

Because

Stale billing data causes support tickets. The whole point was to eliminate the need for a human to clarify.

What I Learned

What I learned leading this work

1

Vision work requires a different kind of courage

Designing for a future state that doesn't have a ticket, a sprint, or a clear owner is uncomfortable. I learned to treat the north star artifact as a persuasion tool first and a spec second — the goal was to make the future feel inevitable, not just desirable.

2

The agent layer exposed gaps in our data model

Designing the AI agent forced conversations about data access that had never happened before. The agent couldn't answer questions about billing if it couldn't read billing state. This design work directly surfaced a platform requirement engineering hadn't prioritized — and got it on the roadmap.

3

Stakeholder alignment is a design deliverable

The most important artifact I made wasn't a prototype — it was a one-page framing document that got four teams into the same room with the same understanding of the problem. I learned to spend more time on alignment artifacts than I naturally wanted to.

4

I underestimated how hard "simple" is

The final portal looks simple. That simplicity took 14 rounds of iteration on the navigation model alone. Making three complex domains feel like one coherent product is the hardest information architecture problem I've worked on — and the most satisfying to ship.

Next Chapter

Where this goes from here

The next phase of this work is making the agent proactive rather than reactive — surfacing billing anomalies, contract renewal reminders, and support trends before the customer has to ask. That requires a different data architecture and a new interaction paradigm: the agent as a persistent presence, not just a support fallback.

The hardest part is designing for the moment when the agent is wrong — how does the portal rebuild trust after a bad answer? That failure-state design is the hardest and most important part we haven't fully solved yet.
