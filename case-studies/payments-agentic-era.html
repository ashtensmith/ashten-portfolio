Payments for the Agentic Era

Making HubSpot's payment links and checkout extensible, adaptable, and observable, for the moment when an AI agent, not a human, initiates the transaction.

The Problem

Payment links were built for humans. Agents don't work that way.

Describe the specific friction, what HubSpot's existing payment links and checkout assumed about who was completing the transaction, and where those assumptions broke down when AI started initiating flows. What did "extensible," "adaptable," and "observable" actually mean in practice, and why were none of those things true?

What were the failure modes: for merchants, for customers, for ops teams trying to understand what happened when an agent-initiated transaction failed or stalled? What was the business cost of this gap?

What made this technically constrained, the existing payments infrastructure, third-party dependencies, or the evolving definition of what "agentic" even meant at the time? This is where you establish the scope of the design challenge.

Key Decisions

Where the hard choices were

Three decisions that defined the design direction, and the reasoning behind each.

Decision 01

Where does checkout configuration live, merchant or agent?

We considered

Letting the agent configure checkout parameters at runtime: dynamic, flexible, but unpredictable for merchants.

We chose

Merchant-set configuration with an agent-readable contract: merchants define the rules, agents operate within them.

Because

Add your reasoning, what trust model, legal constraint, or merchant feedback drove this boundary.

Decision 02

How much does the payer see about who initiated the transaction?

We considered

Full transparency: surfacing "this checkout was initiated by an AI agent" in the payer UI.

We chose

Disclosure without friction: a clear audit trail for merchants, lightweight context for payers where relevant.

Because

Add your reasoning, what payer research, legal guidance, or conversion data informed this balance.

Decision 03

Synchronous confirmation vs. async receipt in agentic flows?

We considered

Requiring synchronous confirmation for every agent-initiated transaction: safe, but blocking for automated flows.

We chose

Async by default with merchant-configurable confirmation gates: agents move fast, merchants set the guardrails.
